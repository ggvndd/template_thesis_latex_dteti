\subsection{Research Gap and Novelty}

From the review of prior studies, it is evident that non-traditional workers in Indonesia face a high risk of burnout, with 41\% of gig workers experiencing income uncertainty and 91\% of part-time students experiencing burnout due to schedule conflicts and algorithmic pressures. Several studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of digital interventions, such as mHealth applications and mood trackers, in reducing stress symptoms by up to 20\%, though most studies remain general or focused on global populations.

The primary gap identified in current literature is the scarcity of localized studies integrating Large Language Model (LLM) based mood trackers with adaptive UI elements—such as context-aware visual simplification and one-tap inputs—specifically designed for young gig workers in Indonesia. Existing research lacks comprehensive evaluation using metrics that encompass both mechanical efficiency (Time on Task) and emotional/personalization aspects (Emotional Response Index and Personalization Effectiveness Index).

Therefore, this study addresses this gap by designing an adaptive UI/UX prototype integrated with the Gemma 3 API, targeted at Universitas Gadjah Mada (UGM) students with side gigs. The evaluation employs a controlled, within-subjects A/B testing approach, measuring three key metrics: Time on Task (ToT) for task efficiency, Emotional Response Index (ERI) for positive emotional impact, and Personalization Effectiveness Index (PEI) for contextual awareness. This targeted approach is expected to provide novel contributions to digital mental health interventions for non-traditional workers in Indonesia.

\section{Theoretical Review}

\subsection{The Problem Space: Gig Economy and Burnout}

\subsubsection{The Gig Economy and Non-Traditional Workers}

The gig economy is a digital platform-based economic model that facilitates temporary or task-based work, where workers earn income from short-term projects without permanent employment ties. According to the International Labour Organization (ILO), the gig economy is characterized by high flexibility and worker autonomy, but also income uncertainty and a lack of social protections. In Indonesia, the number of informal workers (including gig workers and part-timers) reached approximately 86.58 million people in February 2025 (59.4\% of the total workforce according to BPS), with subsets of platform-based gig work—such as freelancing in graphic design, website development, and photography—continuing to grow rapidly among young people. The majority of these workers are young, and many are students who engage in side gigs or part-time jobs to supplement their income.

Non-traditional workers, particularly students, often face a double burden between academic responsibilities and work, leading to high psychological pressure. Key characteristics of gig work include the use of platform algorithms for task allocation, rating systems that influence earnings, and social isolation due to limited face-to-face interactions. These conditions make gig workers vulnerable to mental health issues, such as burnout. This foundational theory serves as the basis for understanding the context of non-traditional workers in Indonesia, which will later be linked to burnout risks and potential digital interventions.

\subsubsection{Burnout Frameworks and Psychological Impact}

Burnout is defined as a psychological syndrome characterized by emotional exhaustion, depersonalization (cynical attitudes toward work), and reduced personal accomplishment (a diminished sense of self-efficacy). The most commonly used framework for measuring burnout is the Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI), developed by Christina Maslach, which assesses these three main dimensions.

In the gig economy, burnout is often triggered by factors such as job insecurity, algorithmic management (pressure from ratings and platform algorithms), and a lack of work-life balance. Studies indicate that gig workers face a 50--60\% higher risk of burnout compared to permanent employees, with symptoms including chronic fatigue and decreased motivation. In Indonesia, students with side gigs or part-time jobs also experience high burnout rates, reaching up to 91\% in some local samples, due to schedule conflicts between studies and work, as well as algorithmic pressures from platforms.

\subsubsection{Clinical Screening for Mental Fatigue (PHQ-9)}

While the Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI) evaluates occupational burnout, evaluating the broader psychological distress of non-traditional workers requires established screening tools. The Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) is a widely validated diagnostic instrument used to measure the severity of depressive symptoms and mental fatigue.

Comprising nine items evaluated on a 4-point Likert scale, the PHQ-9 assesses factors such as sleep disruption, energy loss, and cognitive exhaustion. While a score of 10 or higher indicates moderate-to-severe clinical distress requiring professional intervention, a score below 9 indicates mild to sub-clinical psychological fatigue. In the context of Human-Computer Interaction (HCI), specifically for non-medical self-help applications, targeting users with a PHQ-9 score below 9 is a strict ethical requirement. This ensures the digital intervention is evaluated safely as a preventative tool for early-stage gig-worker burnout, protecting highly vulnerable populations from being tested outside of clinical supervision.

\subsection{Current Digital Interventions}

\subsubsection{mHealth Applications and Mood Trackers}

Mental health refers to a state of emotional, psychological, and social well-being that enables individuals to cope with daily stressors. In the digital era, mobile health (mHealth) applications have become increasingly popular as self-help interventions, including chatbots and mood trackers for emotion journaling and early stress pattern detection. Examples such as Woebot and Wysa use chatbots to provide conversational support and mood tracking, which have been shown to reduce anxiety symptoms by up to 20\% among regular users. Recent trends indicate increased mHealth adoption post-pandemic, particularly for stigma-free self-tracking, with high effectiveness among young populations.

A mood tracker is a feature in mHealth applications that enables users to log their daily emotional states, identify stress triggers, and visualize emotional patterns through simple graphs. This feature is effective for burnout management as it supports self-awareness without requiring direct professional intervention. The primary functions of a mood tracker include:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Emotion logging:} Users select or write their mood condition (e.g., stressed, calm, tired) along with triggers (e.g., work deadlines or traffic jams).
	\item \textbf{Trend visualization:} Displaying data in the form of line graphs or pie charts to observe burnout patterns over specific periods.
	\item \textbf{Basic insights:} Providing simple recommendations based on patterns.
\end{enumerate}

Applications such as Daylio or Moodpath have demonstrated that regular mood tracking can increase self-awareness by up to 25\% among users with chronic stress symptoms. Integration with prebuilt chatbots enables the mood tracker to deliver simple conversational responses.

\subsubsection{Psychological Framework of Digital Interventions}

The conversational agent in this study does not aim to provide clinical therapy or psychiatric diagnosis. Instead, the chatbot's interactions are grounded in the psychological principles of Empathetic Active Listening and simplified Cognitive Behavioral Psychoeducation. Active listening in a digital context involves validating the user's reported emotions without immediate judgment or unsolicited problem-solving, which has been shown to significantly reduce acute emotional distress. Furthermore, by prompting users to identify the specific triggers of their burnout, the chatbot facilitates self-reflection. This aligns with the self-monitoring principles of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), where identifying the relationship between environmental triggers and emotional responses is the first step in effective stress management and emotional regulation for non-traditional workers.

\subsection{Theoretical Foundations of UI/UX}

\subsubsection{Usability Principles and Iterative Design (Nielsen's Heuristics)}

Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) is a field of study that examines the interactions between humans and computer systems, aiming to create usable, useful, and user-friendly interfaces. The usability principles proposed by Jakob Nielsen (1993) serve as a primary foundation in UI/UX design, consisting of ten heuristics.

To apply these heuristics effectively, this study adopts a Literature-Driven Iterative Design approach inspired by human-centered design principles. Traditionally, design cycles involve understanding the context of use, specifying user requirements, designing solutions, and evaluating against those requirements. In this study, the initial understanding of user context and requirements is achieved by synthesizing established academic literature regarding the cognitive load and burnout of non-traditional workers.

\subsubsection{Cognitive Load Theory and Hick's Law}

The necessity for an adaptive interface in this study is theoretically driven by John Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory, specifically the need to minimize extraneous cognitive load—the mental effort required to process poorly designed or overly complex interfaces. For gig workers experiencing burnout, their working memory capacity is already severely depleted.

To address this, the adaptive UI relies heavily on the Hick-Hyman Law (Hick's Law), which states that the time it takes for a person to make a decision increases logarithmically with the number and complexity of choices presented. By intentionally restricting the number of interactive elements, buttons, and navigation menus during high-stress periods, the application minimizes the decision-making time, thereby mathematically reducing the user's extraneous cognitive load and lowering the barrier to daily mood logging.

\subsubsection{Adaptive UI/UX as an Extension of Standard Principles}

Adaptive UI/UX, or a context-aware interface, is an extension of standard usability principles that allows an interface to dynamically adjust itself based on user context, including available time, location, stress levels, physical activity, or input preferences. According to Calvary et al. (2003), this adaptation encompasses the plasticity of user interfaces, where the system can automatically alter its layout to enhance flexibility and efficiency of use.

In the context of non-traditional workers with irregular schedules, adaptations such as switching to one-tap emojis during busy periods, offering voice input while mobile, and simplifying trend visualizations during high stress are crucial for reducing cognitive load and increasing emotional engagement. Relevant adaptation examples for mood trackers include:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Adaptive user interface:} Adjusting the application's layout based on the busy hours the user is currently experiencing (e.g., transitioning to a simplified ``Rush Hour'' mode).
	\item \textbf{Contextual visualization:} Simplifying graphs when users report high stress levels to avoid information overload.
	\item \textbf{Personalized prompts:} Adjusting chatbot questions based on previous mood patterns.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Backend Architecture and System Ethics}

\subsubsection{Large Language Model Architecture via Gemma 3 API}

This study utilizes the Gemma 3 4B model via API as the core conversational engine for Natural Language Understanding (NLU) and response generation. With its massive 128K context window, the Gemma 3 model is capable of maintaining multi-turn conversational context, retaining user mood history, and generating natural, empathetic responses.

When a user submits an input, the mobile client constructs a payload consisting of three elements: (1) the strict System Prompt (acting as the ethical guardrail), (2) the user's recently logged mood data (acting as contextual memory), and (3) the user's raw text input. This payload is processed by the Gemma 3 4B model, allowing the application to parse complex, unstructured human emotion with significantly higher accuracy and conversational fluidity.

\subsubsection{Ethical Considerations in Mental Health Apps}

The development and evaluation of mental health applications must adhere to strict ethical principles, including data privacy, informed consent, and avoiding clinical diagnoses. This study applies guidelines from ISO 9241-210 and WHO recommendations for digital mental health interventions by ensuring all mood data is securely stored in Firebase (end-to-end encryption) and the chatbot is restricted to supportive assistance without therapeutic claims.

This ethics protocol also includes algorithmic guardrails implemented via System Prompting to prevent negative or risky responses. Furthermore, an opt-out mechanism is available at any time, ensuring the research remains safe and responsible for student participants at risk of burnout.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Evaluating UI/UX in mental health applications requires metrics that measure not only mechanical ease of use but also emotional impact. Standard HCI metrics are often insufficient for evaluating highly stressed populations. Therefore, this study employs three specifically justified metrics:

\subsubsection{Interaction Efficiency: Time on Task (ToT)}

Time on Task (ToT) is the standard usability metric for measuring interaction efficiency. Based on the ISO 9241-11 usability framework, efficiency is quantified by the exact duration (in seconds) a user requires to successfully complete a specific scenario. In standard usability testing, Completion Rate is often the primary metric. However, for a mood-tracking application, the interface is simple enough that the completion rate will invariably be near 100\%. Therefore, this study utilizes Time on Task (ToT) as the definitive measure of efficiency. By tracking the exact duration in seconds required to log a mood, ToT acts as a direct proxy for Sweller's Extraneous Cognitive Load. A statistically significant reduction in ToT mathematically proves that the adaptive interface requires less mental effort to navigate than a standard digital interface.

The statistical significance of the ToT difference is calculated using the Paired-samples t-test. The formula used to determine the t-statistic is:
\[t = \frac{\bar{d}}{s_d / \sqrt{n}}\]
where $\bar{d}$ is the mean of the differences between the two UI conditions, $s_d$ is the standard deviation of those differences, and $n$ is the sample size ($N=30$). Practical significance is measured using Cohen's $d_z$ for effect size:
\[d_z = \frac{\bar{d}}{s_d}\]

\subsubsection{Emotional Response Index (ERI)}

To measure the affective success of the application, this study adapted the Emotional Response Index (ERI). Existing clinical metrics, such as the Positive and Negative Affect Schedule (PANAS), are excellent for clinical diagnoses but are too broad to evaluate a 20-second digital interaction. Conversely, standard HCI surveys like the mHealth App Usability Questionnaire (MAUQ) focus heavily on app mechanics rather than user emotions. ERI bridges this gap. By fusing the emotional valence of PANAS with the digital context of MAUQ, ERI specifically evaluates whether the micro-interaction of using the app actively reduced the user's acute frustration, rather than simply measuring if the app was ``easy to use.'' Because ERI is a composite psychometric index derived from Likert-scale questionnaires, its internal consistency is mathematically validated before analysis using Cronbach's Alpha ($\alpha$):
\[\alpha = \frac{k}{k-1} \left( 1 - \frac{\sum \sigma_{y_i}^2}{\sigma_x^2} \right)\]
where $k$ refers to the number of test items, $\sum \sigma_{y_i}^2$ is the sum of the item variances, and $\sigma_x^2$ is the variance of the total composite score.

The evaluation items for ERI measure whether the app actively reduces frustration and promotes calm, rather than just measuring usability. Example items include:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item The application's interface helps me process my emotions without feeling mentally drained.
	\item The simplified visual design of the application makes me feel calm even when I am in a rush.
	\item Interacting with the application during a busy shift did not increase my anxiety or frustration.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Personalization Effectiveness Index (PEI)}

PEI evaluates the system's ability to dynamically align with the user's contextual needs. The evaluation items for this metric are adapted from the Perceived Personalization Scale commonly used in HCI research. Assessed via a 5-point Likert scale, the PEI measures the user's perception of the system's contextual awareness, evaluating factors such as the relevance of the Gemma 3 LLM prompts and the timeliness of the adaptive UI triggers. Like ERI, the reliability of the PEI instrument is validated using the Cronbach's Alpha formula.

While standard Perceived Personalization Scales are commonly used in e-commerce to determine if a system correctly recommends products, gig workers require contextual empathy, not product recommendations. Therefore, the standard scale was adapted into the PEI to measure environmental personalization. PEI evaluates whether the user felt the Gemma 3 LLM and the adaptive triggers accurately understood their temporary environmental constraints (e.g., being in a rush, feeling physically exhausted), proving the system acts as a context-aware companion rather than a static tool. Example items include:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item The ``Rush Hour'' interface accurately anticipates my need for a faster input method during my busy shifts.
	\item The chatbot's responses felt specifically tailored to my unique mood history rather than being generic or automated.
	\item Interface successfully hides unnecessary features and only shows me what I need.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Adaptive UI/UX as an Extension of Standard Principles}

Although Nielsen's usability heuristics and foundational design principles provide a strong baseline, users with high cognitive demands---such as non-traditional workers---require the development of adaptive UI/UX. Adaptive UI dynamically adjusts the interface based on user context, such as available time, stress levels, or input preferences \cite{pmc2025scopingreview}. Relevant adaptation examples for mood trackers include:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Adaptive user interface:} Adjusting the application's user interface based on the user's current busy hours (e.g., transitioning to a simplified ``Rush Hour'' mode).
	\item \textbf{Contextual visualization:} Simplifying graphs when users report high stress levels to avoid information overload.
	\item \textbf{Personalized prompts:} Adjusting chatbot questions based on previous mood patterns (e.g., ``Feeling tired after last night's side gig, huh?'').
\end{itemize}

This adaptive approach remains grounded in Nielsen's usability principles (e.g., flexibility and efficiency of use) and iterative design, while adding a contextual layer to enhance effectiveness for users with specific characteristics like non-traditional workers \cite{jmir2025digitalinterventions}. This concept addresses gaps in existing mHealth applications, which often lack adaptability to the cognitive constraints of gig workers, as will be further discussed in the review of prior studies.

\subsection{Supporting Technology: Gemma 3 LLM Architecture}

This study utilizes the Gemma 3 4B model via API as the core conversational engine for Natural Language Understanding (NLU) and response generation. The architecture operates on a dynamic prompt-engineering framework rather than a rigid intent-matching structure.

With its massive 128K context window, the Gemma 3 model is capable of maintaining multi-turn conversational context, retaining user mood history, and generating natural, empathetic, and highly contextual responses. Unlike traditional intent-based chatbots (such as Dialogflow or Rasa) that rely on strict regular expressions or pre-defined conversational flows, the LLM approach allows the system to process unstructured user input flexibly \cite{oulasvirta2024personalization}.

This architecture is highly suitable for providing basic insights based on the user's mood patterns, generating supportive responses such as ``Looking at your trend tonight, it seems you're exhausted after your side gig shift---would you like to talk more about it?'' The Gemma 3 4B API was chosen for its lightweight efficiency for mobile integration, allowing rapid iteration within the iterative design approach while still providing advanced NLU capabilities.

When a user submits an input, the mobile client constructs a payload consisting of three elements: (1) the strict System Prompt (acting as the ethical guardrail and defining the bot's empathetic persona), (2) the user's recently logged mood data (acting as contextual memory), and (3) the user's raw text input. This payload is processed by the Gemma 3 4B model, which leverages its extensive context window to generate a highly personalized, natural language response. This architecture completely replaces modular, regular expression (regex) rule-based systems, allowing the application to parse complex, unstructured human emotion with significantly higher accuracy and conversational fluidity.

\subsection{Psychological Framework of the Chatbot Intervention}

The conversational agent in this study does not aim to provide clinical therapy or psychiatric diagnosis. Instead, the chatbot's interactions are grounded in the psychological principles of Empathetic Active Listening and simplified Cognitive Behavioral Psychoeducation. Active listening in a digital context involves validating the user's reported emotions without immediate judgment or unsolicited problem-solving, which has been shown to significantly reduce acute emotional distress \cite{miner2016chatbot}. Furthermore, by prompting users to identify the specific triggers of their burnout (e.g., ``What made you feel stressed during your shift today?''), the chatbot facilitates self-reflection. This aligns with the self-monitoring principles of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), where identifying the relationship between environmental triggers and emotional responses is the first step in effective stress management and emotional regulation for non-traditional workers \cite{beck2020cbt}.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations in Mental Health Applications}

The development and evaluation of mental health applications must adhere to strict ethical principles, including data privacy (encryption and anonymity), informed consent, avoiding clinical diagnoses, and avoiding responses that could be harmful or create dependency \cite{who2023ethics, torous2021standards}. This study applies guidelines from ISO 9241-210 and WHO recommendations for digital mental health interventions by ensuring all mood data is securely stored in Firebase (end-to-end encryption), users are given clear explanations through informed consent, and the chatbot is restricted to supportive assistance without therapeutic claims.

This ethics protocol also includes algorithmic guardrails implemented via System Prompting on the Gemma 3 API to prevent negative or risky responses. If preprocessing filters detect critical trigger words indicating severe psychological distress, the LLM generation is strictly bypassed, and a hardcoded mechanism provides direct referrals to UGM psychological counseling services and emergency hotline numbers. Furthermore, an opt-out mechanism is available at any time, ensuring the research remains safe and responsible for student participants at risk of burnout.

\section{Conclusion on Literature Review}

The comprehensive review of literature from 2019--2025 reveals that gig workers and non-traditional workers in Indonesia face significant burnout risks, exacerbated by algorithmic control, income uncertainty, and schedule conflicts. While existing digital interventions (mHealth apps, mood trackers, chatbots) show promise in reducing stress by up to 20\%, current research lacks localized evaluations of adaptive UI/UX specifically designed for young Indonesian gig workers with high cognitive load.

This study bridges this gap by proposing an adaptive UI/UX mood tracker integrated with the Gemma 3 API, evaluated using three theoretically justified metrics: Time on Task (ToT) for efficiency, Emotional Response Index (ERI) for affective impact, and Personalization Effectiveness Index (PEI) for contextual awareness. The evaluation design combines in-person controlled sessions with 7-day asynchronous usage to capture both immediate and sustained effects. By grounding the intervention in established theoretical frameworks (Cognitive Load Theory, Hick's Law, CBT principles, and ISO standards), this research is expected to provide evidence-based contributions to digital mental health support for non-traditional workers in Indonesia.
